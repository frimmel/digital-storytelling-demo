l'eau de vie
Increased growth in the Southern Appalachians, and a resulting increase in development, places strain on both the quantity and the quality of the region's natural assets—including water.

The Southern Appalachian Mountains—which are among the oldest mountains on Earth—are unique in the world both in their natural beauty, the resources they provide, and their cultural heritage.

The region's geology and climate contribute to its scenic landscapes, healthy natural environments, rich biological diversity, and connections to the past.

A key element underlying all of these features is water.

The value of water in the Southern Appalachians is of vast importance. The first—and perhaps most obvious—benefit of the teeming streams, rivers, and lakes in these mountains is the ecosystem services they provide: clean drinking water, tourism and recreation potential, and high-quality habitat for freshwater fish and other aquatic organisms.

From 2000 to 2010, the region's human population has grown by a mere 7-percent—a rate that is expected to accelerate in future decades—but a rate also not consistently distributed throughout the region. Likely due to economic pressures, much of the region's rural areas have seen a decline in their populations while residents have relocated to more urban areas along or near major metropolitan corridors. This increased growth, and the resulting increase in development in these areas, places strain on both the quantity and the quality of the region's natural assets—including water.
